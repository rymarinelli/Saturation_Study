# Data

Our primary data source is the Epoch AI Benchmarking Hub (Epoch AI, 2026), a curated database of AI benchmark results released under a Creative Commons Attribution (CC-BY 4.0) license, retrieved as the bulk CSV export on 14 July 2026 (data last updated 7 July 2026). The Hub combines two classes of results. The first consists of evaluations run internally by Epoch AI using the UK AI Security Institute's Inspect framework, covering benchmarks such as GPQA Diamond, MATH Level 5, FrontierMath, SWE-bench Verified, and OTIS Mock AIME; for these, task definitions and evaluation logs are published alongside the scores. The second consists of externally sourced results collected from developer documentation — model cards, system cards, and technical reports — as well as official benchmark leaderboards (e.g., the Cybench leaderboard, Scale AI's SEAL leaderboards, and the Stanford CRFM HELM leaderboard). Each record includes the model version, the score under the benchmark's primary metric, the model's public release date, the developing organization, and, where applicable, a link to the primary source. We supplement the Hub's cyber-offense coverage with scores hand-collected from Anthropic and OpenAI system cards for CyberGym and OpenAI's internal cyber-range evaluation, which we digitized directly from the published documents.

From the raw export we retain the 54 benchmarks whose primary metric is a bounded proportion (accuracy, pass rate, or percent solved), normalizing all scores to the unit interval; benchmarks reported on unbounded or relative scales — Elo-style arena ratings, METR's task-completion time horizons, and speedup-ratio metrics — are excluded from the saturation analysis, as the notion of a 100% ceiling does not apply. After normalization the dataset comprises approximately 3,400 model–benchmark observations. For each benchmark we construct the state-of-the-art (SOTA) frontier as the running maximum of reported scores ordered by model release date, and resample this step function to a regular monthly grid by forward-filling. The resulting object of analysis is a collection of 54 discretely observed trajectories — one function per benchmark, mapping calendar time to frontier performance — which we treat both as inputs to parametric curve fitting and as curve-valued observations in a functional data analysis. We date each benchmark's entry into the dataset by its first recorded frontier score; for benchmarks predating the Hub's coverage this may lag the true release date by several months, a limitation we return to below.

A structural feature of these data, central to the choice of methods, is that the trajectories are only partially observed as functions. Benchmarks enter the record at different stages of their progress curves — some already above the 50% midpoint, others still far below it — and the most recent benchmarks are right-censored well before saturation. Of the 35 trajectories that cross the 50% midpoint, only seven are observed over even a modest common window of eighteen months around that crossing. Any analysis of trajectory shape must therefore either impose a parametric form to bridge the unobserved segments or accept substantially reduced samples when working nonparametrically; we do both and report the methods' agreement.

Two further properties of the data warrant caution. First, externally sourced scores are heterogeneous in elicitation: developer-reported figures typically reflect vendor-optimized scaffolds and sampling budgets (e.g., pass@k regimes that vary across system cards), whereas Epoch's internal evaluations use a standardized harness; within-benchmark comparisons therefore mix measurement conditions. Second, the frontier construction is robust to individual noisy scores only from below — a single inflated report permanently raises the recorded SOTA. Where the Hub flags results as non-comparable across benchmark versions (e.g., Terminal-Bench 2.0 versus 2.1), we treat the versions as distinct series.

# Methods

We measure the speed of benchmark saturation in three complementary ways, ordered from strongest to weakest structural assumptions.

First, a parametric approach: for each benchmark with at least twelve monthly observations we fit three-parameter logistic and Gompertz functions to the SOTA trajectory, with a free ceiling bounded above by 100%, retaining the better-fitting form by root-mean-square error. The fitted rate parameter k summarizes the steepness of the sigmoid, and the fitted crossing of 95% of the ceiling defines a projected saturation date for benchmarks that have not yet saturated empirically.

Second, a functional data analysis that avoids committing to a sigmoid family. We landmark-register the trajectories by re-anchoring each at its linearly interpolated 50% crossing, represent the registered curves in a cubic B-spline basis (seven basis functions), and apply functional principal component analysis (FPCA) to the subset of curves fully observed on a common window of [−12, +9] months around the landmark. FPCA decomposes the sample of curves into a functional mean and orthogonal modes of variation, letting the data determine which aspects of trajectory shape vary most across benchmarks.

Third, a fully nonparametric summary that tolerates partial observation: for each benchmark whose frontier has passed 70% — a threshold chosen so that the steepest segment of the trajectory has plausibly been observed — we compute the peak saturation velocity, defined as the maximum month-over-month gain of the lightly smoothed SOTA series, in percentage points per month. Unlike the registered FPCA, this statistic requires no common observation window and is available for 27 benchmarks.

Saturation throughout is operationalized as the SOTA frontier reaching 95% of the maximum attainable score, and a benchmark's lifespan as the interval from its first frontier score to that crossing, empirical where observed and fit-implied otherwise.

# Findings: the accelerating rate of benchmark saturation

Benchmark lifespans are contracting. Across the 39 benchmarks with a realized or projected saturation date before 2030, regressing lifespan on the benchmark's entry date yields a slope of −5.4 months per calendar year (r = −0.53, p < 0.001): each successive year of benchmark introduction is associated with roughly five and a half months less useful life. Restricting the regression to the six benchmarks whose 95% crossing is directly observed — removing any dependence on extrapolation — steepens the estimate to −7.5 months per year (r = −0.91, p = 0.011). In cohort terms, benchmarks first scored before 2023 took a median of 4.7 years to saturate; the 2023 and 2024 cohorts took approximately 2.7 years; and the post-2025 cohort is on track for roughly 2.3 years, with recent extremes substantially lower (Terminal-Bench, approximately 13 months; FictionLiveBench, under four months from first score to saturation).

The contraction is driven by steepening progress curves, and this steepening is visible under all three methods, with confidence commensurate to their assumptions. Under the parametric fits, log(k) increases by approximately 0.30 per year of benchmark introduction (r = 0.57, p = 0.0001), implying that the characteristic steepness of progress curves multiplies by roughly 1.35× per year; the median fitted rate rises from k = 0.52 for pre-2023 benchmarks to k ≈ 2.6 for the 2023–2024 cohort, a five-fold steepening. The functional analysis supports the premise of this comparison: the leading principal component of the registered trajectories explains 71% of the variation in curve shape and correlates at −0.87 with within-window gain — that is, the dominant axis along which benchmark trajectories differ is precisely their steepness, confirming model-free that rate is the natural object of study rather than an artifact of the sigmoid family. The association between this steepness mode and introduction date runs in the expected direction but is underpowered on the seven fully observed curves (r = 0.42, p = 0.35). The nonparametric peak-velocity measure, available for 27 benchmarks, shows cohort medians rising from 5.9 points per month (pre-2023) to 7.0 (2023) to 10.4 (2024 onward); the corresponding regression of log peak velocity on introduction year gives approximately 1.09× per year but does not reach significance (p = 0.15). The same pattern appears in a fourth, purely descriptive cut: aligned at their 50% crossings, benchmarks introduced from 2024 onward gain a median of 62 percentage points over the ±12-month window around the midpoint, against 52 points for earlier benchmarks. We read this ensemble as triangulation: the strongly parametric estimate is statistically decisive, the weakly parametric and nonparametric estimates are individually underpowered but uniformly concordant in sign and magnitude, and no method points the other way.

Three considerations bound the interpretation of these estimates. First, the sample is right-censored in a way that classical estimators handle poorly: a benchmark introduced in 2025 that will ultimately take six years to saturate cannot yet appear as a slow observation, mechanically truncating recent cohorts from above, and the same censoring is what reduces the registered FPCA to seven complete curves. Both problems have a common remedy — treating the trajectories as sparsely observed functional data and estimating principal components from the pooled covariance surface of curve fragments (Yao et al., 2005), or equivalently fitting a hierarchical functional model in which each benchmark contributes whatever segment it has, with time-to-saturation handled as a censored outcome in the survival-analytic sense. We regard this as the principal methodological extension of the present work. Second, the trend is measured against a backdrop of deliberately escalating benchmark difficulty: successor benchmarks such as FrontierMath, Humanity's Last Exam, and ARC-AGI-2 were explicitly constructed to resist saturation, yet exhibit shorter projected lifespans than their easier predecessors, so the contraction in lifespan understates the underlying acceleration in capability gain. Third, part of the steepening reflects the maturation of the evaluation ecosystem rather than model capability alone: newly introduced benchmarks launch into an environment of well-developed agent scaffolds, elicitation practice, and evaluation-aware training pipelines, and so begin partway up the slope earlier benchmarks had to climb from scratch. Under any decomposition, however, the operational conclusion is unchanged: the useful life of a static benchmark at the frontier has roughly halved since 2022, and evaluation regimes — including capability thresholds embedded in safety frameworks — must be versioned and re-anchored on a correspondingly shorter cycle.
