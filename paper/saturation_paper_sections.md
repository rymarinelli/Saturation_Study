# Data

Our primary data source is the Epoch AI Benchmarking Hub (Epoch AI, 2026), a curated database of AI benchmark results released under a Creative Commons Attribution (CC-BY 4.0) license, retrieved as the bulk CSV export on 14 July 2026 (data last updated 7 July 2026). The Hub combines two classes of results. The first consists of evaluations run internally by Epoch AI using the UK AI Security Institute's Inspect framework, covering benchmarks such as GPQA Diamond, MATH Level 5, FrontierMath, SWE-bench Verified, and OTIS Mock AIME; for these, task definitions and evaluation logs are published alongside the scores. The second consists of externally sourced results collected from developer documentation — model cards, system cards, and technical reports — as well as official benchmark leaderboards (e.g., the Cybench leaderboard, Scale AI's SEAL leaderboards, and the Stanford CRFM HELM leaderboard). Each record includes the model version, the score under the benchmark's primary metric, the model's public release date, the developing organization, and, where applicable, a link to the primary source. We supplement the Hub's cyber-offense coverage with scores hand-collected from Anthropic and OpenAI system cards for CyberGym and OpenAI's internal cyber-range evaluation, which we digitized directly from the published documents.

From the raw export we retain the 54 benchmarks whose primary metric is a bounded proportion (accuracy, pass rate, or percent solved), normalizing all scores to the unit interval; benchmarks reported on unbounded or relative scales — Elo-style arena ratings, METR's task-completion time horizons, and speedup-ratio metrics — are excluded from the saturation analysis, as the notion of a 100% ceiling does not apply. After normalization the dataset comprises approximately 3,400 model–benchmark observations. For each benchmark we construct the state-of-the-art (SOTA) frontier as the running maximum of reported scores ordered by model release date, and resample this step function to a regular monthly grid by forward-filling, yielding a balanced panel suitable for curve fitting. We date each benchmark's entry into the dataset by its first recorded frontier score; for benchmarks predating the Hub's coverage this may lag the true release date by several months, a limitation we return to below.

Two properties of the data warrant caution. First, externally sourced scores are heterogeneous in elicitation: developer-reported figures typically reflect vendor-optimized scaffolds and sampling budgets (e.g., pass@k regimes that vary across system cards), whereas Epoch's internal evaluations use a standardized harness; within-benchmark comparisons therefore mix measurement conditions. Second, the frontier construction is robust to individual noisy scores only from below — a single inflated report permanently raises the recorded SOTA. Where the Hub flags results as non-comparable across benchmark versions (e.g., Terminal-Bench 2.0 versus 2.1), we treat the versions as distinct series.

# Results: the accelerating rate of benchmark saturation

We operationalize saturation as the date at which a benchmark's SOTA frontier first reaches 95% of the maximum attainable score, using the empirical crossing where it has occurred and the crossing implied by a fitted sigmoid where it has not. Defining a benchmark's lifespan as the interval from its first frontier score to its saturation date, we find that lifespans are contracting markedly. Across the 39 benchmarks with a realized or projected saturation date before 2030, regressing lifespan on the benchmark's entry date yields a slope of −5.4 months per calendar year (r = −0.53, p < 0.001): each successive year of benchmark introduction is associated with roughly five and a half months less useful life. Restricting the regression to the six benchmarks whose 95% crossing is directly observed — thereby removing any dependence on extrapolation — steepens the estimate to −7.5 months per year (r = −0.91, p = 0.011). In cohort terms, benchmarks first scored before 2023 took a median of 4.7 years to saturate; the 2023 and 2024 cohorts took approximately 2.7 years; and the post-2025 cohort is on track for roughly 2.3 years, with recent extremes substantially lower (Terminal-Bench, approximately 13 months; FictionLiveBench, under four months from first score to saturation).

A complementary measure of saturation speed comes from the shape of the progress curves themselves. For each benchmark with at least twelve monthly observations we fit both a three-parameter logistic and a three-parameter Gompertz function to the SOTA series, with a free ceiling bounded above by 100%, and retain the better-fitting form by root-mean-square error. The fitted rate parameter k, which governs the steepness of the sigmoid, rises systematically with benchmark introduction date: log(k) increases by approximately 0.30 per year (r = 0.57, p = 0.0001), implying that the characteristic steepness of benchmark progress curves multiplies by roughly 1.35× per year. The median fitted rate for benchmarks introduced before 2023 is k = 0.52, against k ≈ 2.6 for the 2023–2024 cohort — a five-fold steepening. The same pattern is visible non-parametrically: aligning all frontiers at their interpolated 50% crossing, the median SOTA gain in the ±12-month window around the midpoint is 52 percentage points for pre-2024 benchmarks and 62 points for those introduced later.

Three considerations bound the interpretation of these estimates. First, the sample of unsaturated benchmarks is right-censored: a benchmark introduced in 2025 that will ultimately take six years to saturate cannot yet appear as a slow observation, mechanically truncating recent cohorts from above. Our reliance on projected crossings for these benchmarks partially mitigates but does not eliminate this bias, and a survival-analytic treatment — modeling time-to-saturation with unsaturated benchmarks as censored observations — is the appropriate framework for future work. Second, the trend is measured against a backdrop of deliberately escalating benchmark difficulty: successor benchmarks such as FrontierMath, Humanity's Last Exam, and ARC-AGI-2 were explicitly constructed to resist saturation, yet exhibit shorter projected lifespans than their easier predecessors. The contraction in lifespan therefore understates the underlying acceleration in capability gain, since it is achieved despite a rising difficulty bar. Third, part of the steepening reflects the maturation of the evaluation ecosystem rather than model capability alone: newly introduced benchmarks now launch into an environment of well-developed agent scaffolds, elicitation practice, and evaluation-aware training pipelines, so their progress curves begin partway up the slope that earlier benchmarks had to climb from scratch. Disentangling these ecosystem effects from model-level capability growth is a natural extension, but under any decomposition the operational conclusion is the same: the useful life of a static benchmark at the frontier has roughly halved since 2022, and evaluation regimes — including capability thresholds embedded in safety frameworks — must be versioned and re-anchored on a correspondingly shorter cycle.
